2019

torch mk1

The Plan:

OK, this was very fun, was and still is my favorite project. There is something about holding a device that is 100 times more powerful then you have ever held.

This project started with me watching DIY perks’ "Make a 1000w equiv. LED flashlight - aka DIY Sun-Blaster!" video. In the video he made a torch that used a 100W LED and a CPU heat sink. credit to him his design was simple, powerful and above all else cheap.

I grabbed an old-fashioned pencil and graph paper and designed my torch. I added A/C powered charging, automatic power switching that reroutes the lights power from the battery to the power supply when charging via relay logic. After 10 minutes and a generous helping of over engineering I found myself with this:

The Buy

Next I did the next logical thing that any of my teenage friends who are girls would do in a heartbeat, shopping. But not the teenage girl kind. To cut this year long project story short. I found all the parts I needed on eBay and Amazon, wasn't hard at all. However, there is problem with eBay/amazon li-ion batteries. I didn't want to get scammed with rubbish batteries that turned my house into the memorial my house once stood. In the end I found nkon.nl which was an honest Dutch battery distributor. I went with Samsung's INR18650-30Q with 3Ah (300mAh) of charge and a continuous discharge of 15A. I used 4 of them in series to give me 14.4v @ 15A for 3Ah, this totals to 216W (max) for 43Whr.

Side note: honestly, I feel sorry for the lass that I will (hopefully and or eventually) find because this is shopping not any outfit nonsense.

In summary for the parts, the only thing that was sketchy was the power supply which was a 12v 200W PSU. It worked fine and I even had a look on the inside it had 6+ grounding points on the PCB that contact the grounded chassis and had A+ AC/DC isolation (Back in Black is my favourite). but it had wire terminals, so I had to work with a death cable (what, you don't know what that is? It’s a power cord with exposed live, neutral and depending on your country ground wires).

"A/C and Lithium battery? Don't you think that's a bad idea?" I hear you think. Well yes, I have never powered up the torch with AC apart from on a safe workbench with absolutely nobody in the danger zone (exclude the joke about the song because I am trying to make a serious point). Don't mess with A/C power unless you are a pro, or you put the right precautions in place and know what you are doing. In the end, I did not include an A/C plug on my torch as it's dangerous and I'm quite fond of living.

The build:

The heat sink and LED arrived the next day because Amazon prime is awesome. Putting the two together was surprisingly easy. Like cheese on toast, I had to make zero hack modifications to the heat sink to get the two introduced. Even the hole in the bottom bracket was perfectly big (hey, I’m a glass half full guy). The next day a voltage regulator came in the post, within 5 minutes I had both the fan and LED hooked up.

Testing time (my power supply only goes up to 31V so this test is at half power).:

The next lot of parts that arrived were to do with the battery. I’m not going to lie it’s a little bit scary working with li-ion batteries. If you get them over 60°C it’s broken. I’m going to use 350°C solder on them, so I had to be quick. In the end I was very happy with the results. I used silver copper wire to connect the batteries together and added a BMS (battery management system) to it because these batteries where expensive and I didn’t want to break them:

Well it was quite a wait after that until anything else happened. But then something did happen. Once I finished recieving all the parts I had to design a circuit board that would bring all the parts together. I am going to be honest I should have designed it properly before building it buuuuttt I winged it. To be honest it was only relay logic. It wasn’t going to be that hard and it wasn’t, but I could’ve been more efficient on space. However, when your torch is 8'' long, space isn't a problem.

The assemble

I went to the nearest material store and picked up an Aluminium angle. With a sharpie in one hand and ruler the other I marked out all the cuts I had to make. 5 Minutes and I was done. The cuts on the other hand took forever. My hacksaw was very blunt and very short. So around an hour and a half was required for this step, but now I have all the parts for the frame:

The rest of the assemble was easy I used rivets and bolts to secure the frame together. To be truthful I did not measure the size of the components that needed to go inside of the torch. So getting everything inside was a squash but still possible.

Summary

In summary I have a not so compact torch that Is brighter than anyone ever needs.

It’s pretty cool though!
